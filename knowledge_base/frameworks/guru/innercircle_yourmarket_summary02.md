YOUR MARKET: Session 2
Connecting With Your Market
In this session, you'll learn how to create an entirely new niche of your own, the importance of
creating real value for your customers, and how to avoid the #1 mistake business owners make.

• One of the most important things you can do as a business owner to creating a new category for
your product. A new niche. Not copying someone else's. Creating a new one. These new
categories or niches exist in the mind of the customer. We're not creating new categories from our
mind, and then telling the customer about it... we're identifying a new category in the mind of the
customer. The method we'll use to create new categories is to look for unmet needs the customer
believes she has.

• As we do this, it's critical to remember most customers can't even comprehend business oﬀers.
Most of your prospects don't even "get" what it is you're selling. We tend to only get things that
directly connect to the need we think we have, so make sure to link everything you're doing back
to the needs and wants the customer believes she has.

• There are two basic business approaches. The first I call Make Money. In this approach the
business owner does whatever he needs to to make more money. There's no conscience about
social, environmental, or moral issues. But there's another philosophy very distinct from this one. I
call it Create Value. Get Money is about get. Create Value is about give. Get Money is about giving
away the minimum to get them to give me money. Create Value is about creating the maximum
amount of value and figuring out how to market it. Get Money isn't sustainable. Create Value is.

• Of course, you can't give away everything and expect to have a business. Money is the oxygen of
business. There must be balance.

• In communicating to our prospect, it's important to understand that most of our decisions - and
particularly our BUYING decisions - are not rational. They are made beyond our conscious
awareness. If you're talking about your market in rational terms, you're oﬀ track. Instead, look for
your customers unconscious drives and keep looking until they becomes obvious to you.

• I good friend of mine and brilliant marketer, Joe Polish, says that sometimes it's useful to think of
your product as glop. We tend to fall in love with our products and get attached, but to our
customer it's just glop. They have no idea what it really is. You must work on their perceptions.

• As you're working on their perceptions, there's a critical diﬀerence between the message vs. the
delivery vehicle. Many business owners spend a lot of their time deciding what delivery vehicle
they should use - should they use banners, or email, or direct mail, or social media, etc. But that's
actually secondary. The most important piece is the message itself, regardless of the media you're
advertising in. It's about understanding the customer and matching the pitch to the customer's
needs.

• Most marketing that I look at is about the product or service or the company creating it, and not
about the customer. There's an ego thing here. So as you're describing your niche/ category, the
first word uttered from your mouth should be about your customer, not you. The unique USP's of
FedEx and Domino's Pizza are pinnacle examples of this. We can't analyze them enough. "Fresh,
hot pizza delivered in 30 minutes, guaranteed." "When it absolutely, positively has to get there
overnight." These aren't about the companies - they're about the customer's needs and desires.
