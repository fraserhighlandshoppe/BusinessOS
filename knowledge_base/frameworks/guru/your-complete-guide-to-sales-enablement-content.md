Introduction
When marketing teams and sales organizations work well together, they
provide valuable insights for each team to support each other. Which
content strategies are really working to attract prospects and leads? What
messaging should the sales team be using to best represent the product
or service? That’s where sales enablement comes in!
Every successful deal begins with tailoring your content to your customer’s
journey–providing the sales team with the right tools to use with the right
audiences at the right time allows them engage their prospects in a more
consultative and customized fashion.
In this eBook, you will find a complete guide to sales enablement strategy to
help align your marketing and sales teams, and ultimately, provide relevant
content to your sales organization. Below, we’ll dive into sales enablement
best practices, sales enablement tools, and how to build the right content to
help close deals.

What is Sales Enablement
and Why is it Important?
Sales enablement helps to better align your sales process with the buyer’s
journey, improve the training process for new sales reps, and reduce the
length of the sales cycle. Sales enablement, when done well, can positively
impact company’s bottom line by contributing to Monthly Recurring
Revenue, or MRR. It’s how sales teams access relevant content, tools, and
strategies to drive conversations with their prospects, from start to close.
Materials can be created by teams who are responsible for providing air
cover to sales–it may be a marketing team or a team dedicated to sales
enablement. Sales enablement generally entails:
Generating awareness for your product in order to produce
inbound leads
Working cross-functionally to create educational materials
about your product
Ensuring that sales reps have the most up-to-date messaging
on your product
Assisting the sales process by creating the tools they need
to get to the finish line
Communicating how to use content to various stages of the
sales funnel
Segmenting and tailoring content to your various audiences
and use cases

How to Build a Sales Enablement Process
Before you begin creating sales enablement content, remember that this an
ongoing process and your objective in this process isn’t only to close deals.
It’s also to close the feedback loop between marketing and sales. Before
you create any materials, talk to your sales team. Don’t fall into the trap of
thinking you know exactly what your sales organization needs. Have a
meeting with the leader of your sales organization or conduct a focus group
with your sales reps (or several, depending on the size of your team) in order
to understand content gaps, knowledge gaps and how they prioritize
their needs.
In order to determine where there are holes in your sales enablement
strategy, here are a few questions that can aid discovery:
Is there a single source of truth among the team where messaging,
talk tracks, phone scripts and sales content are organized?
How are new members of the team being onboarded?
Are sales reps currently referencing hacked together documents
or spreadsheets that you’ve never seen during sales calls?
Are they using months or even years old content during sales calls?
Are they repurposing content at various stages of the sales funnel?
Are they directing the same content at widely different audiences?
Have they been requesting tools through the wrong channels?
These questions can help you determine where to start in building your
sales enablement engine. Your sales team should have a single source of

How Sales Enablement Helps the
Marketing Team
Creating sales enablement content is a great way for marketers to take a
look at the most important aspects of the product or service that your
company offers. Think of it as an expansion on the elevator pitch. If a sales
rep has someone on the phone or in person for 15 minutes, what are the
most critical things that you want them to relay? Sales enablement content
provides an opportunity to create clearly defined, succinct positioning of
the product/service.
Sales enablement allows marketing teams to maintain deep knowledge of
the sales process, the customers, their pain points, and positioning for your
organization’s products. By creating a channel for conversation between
sales and marketing, both teams can learn and grow together. Sales teams
are at the front lines of the marketplace—they’re learning directly from the
customer, and they can provide valuable insights that allow the marketing
team to better tailor their materials.

9

How Sales Enablement Helps the Sales Team
Sales enablement helps sales reps have easy access to the most up-to-date
positioning, language, and terminology on your product/service. Rather
than finding out that sales reps have been using a one-pager that they dug
up on the shared drive from 2008 (this is more common than you think),
make sales enablement materials accessible, convenient, and easy to use.
Sales enablement allows the sales team to focus on their customers and
conversations—with the right discovery and a library of tools to utilize
when needed, they can continue to answer the customer’s needs.
For newer sales reps, sales managers can use this content as a good starting
point during sales training to quickly get them up and running. If you
haven’t created a sales process yet, or need some updating (has it really
been since 2008 since you’ve looked at your sales process?), check out this
guide on creating a flexible sales process to drive exponential growth.
For high performing sales teams, it’s critical to stay organized and have all
of the information they need about a prospect easy to find and store. The
HubSpot CRM works for both large and small sales teams, organizing
prospects and leads, and keeping sales enablement information on hand
for each prospect—so you know exactly who viewed which content piece.

When Sales and Marketing Work Together
Creating effective channels for communication is crucial to success. Messaging tools like Slack allow you to share sales enablement materials in real
time, while analytics tools like Docsend allow you to assess the performance
of your sales tools, in order to iterate on them. Airtable bases can provide
that single source of truth, where members of the team can not only find
the right materials, but note what worked well and what didn’t–remember,
sales enablement is about continually refining and improving the sales
process. If you don’t already have a sales and marketing alignment meeting
in place, you can begin a “smarketing” meeting. By holding each other
accountable, both teams are motivated to maintain communication and
provide essential feedback to each other.
Let’s dive into the different types of effective sales enablement content,
how to create them, and when and how they should be used by sales reps.

Product/Service One-Pagers
What they’re all about
A one-pager about your company is a quick way to educate customers on
your product offerings. It is a good tool to use as a leave-behind for your
customers or have your sales reps attach them to a follow-up email after
an initial phone call.
How to make them
Work cross functionally with your product and design team to have the
most up-to-date information about your product or service. Make sure to
package the information in a visually appealing format that will engage
the customer, and conveys your brand identity well. A one-pager provides
high-level overview, no need to go into extremely specific detail here! Your
customers are busy, and expect your sales materials to answer their
questions as succinctly as possible. If you’re finding trouble fitting all of
your information into one page, consider the possibility that you need to
be more granular in the aspect of your product that you’re covering.
Consult your sales and product teams to create thematic chunks of your
product—each aspect may be worthy of its own one-pager.
One-pagers can cover product offerings, frame your product around a
particular industry or use case, or announce upcoming opportunities
and milestones. The one pagers you produce will depend on the nature
of your business.

Case Studies/Testimonials
What they’re all about
Social proof is a vital part of every sales conversation. Almost 70% of
consumers check reviews before making a purchase, with the highest
percentage of opinion seekers searching for reviews before buying technology
products. Your customers can serve as your best advocates, and having
testimonials and case studies available in various industries will help
sales reps have tailored conversations with any potential buyers. Build
a library of case studies that showcases all of your use cases, industries,
company types and most importantly, how your product has helped your
customers succeed.
How to make them
Create a questionnaire that your successful clients can easily fill out: ask
them to share, in their own words, information about their company,
mission, product, and team. Ask about their goals, and the difficulties they
may have encountered in achieving them, as well as how your product has
helped these efforts. Include actual results when possible—data is compelling
for new clients, particularly if your company can help them improve sales,
save money, or help others.
Include your sales reps in the process. Educate your sales team on how to
get client agreement for case studies, as well as the case study process, in
order to field client concerns. Ask members of your sales team, support
team, or customer success team for particularly excited clients who have
found success with your company, and proactively reach out for a case study
or testimonial.

Phone Scripts/Email Templates
What they’re all about
Phone scripts and email templates can be used for many different
cases within the sales cycle. You can create scripts for new product features,
events, or tools, or simply main points to be featured on initial or follow-up
calls. Phone scripts allow the marketing or sales enablement team to have
control over language and messaging used with potential clients. Email
templates are handy tools to expedite the sales process with pre-written
follow up emails, outreach emails, and a version of your product/service
one-pager (main features, positioning statement, and social proof) to send
after phone calls.
Create an automated system for generating outreach or follow-up emails so
your team simply to has to pick the best option from a list, customize it to
their client’s needs, and hit send. This will save your sales team time and
allow for the sales enablement team to easily update messaging with
product/service changes.
How to make them
For phone scripts, although it may sound counterintuitive, try not to write
out every word your sales reps will say. This will make them sound
robotic and not authentic. Include bullet points and conversation topics,
as well as main product features and frequently asked questions. Try to
link pain points with product features, and highlight how you can solve
prospects’ problems. Here’s a full guide to creating sales scripts. When it
comes to arming your sales team with the best tools out there, phone scripts
should be more like phone call conversation starters. Try coming up with a

To create email templates, first consider the phase in the sales process.
(For all phases in the sales process, use this guide to creating sales email
templates.) Start out with a compelling subject line that isn’t too salesy.
Try something like “Question about [problem they may have]” or “Thoughts

You can tweak email templates for different use cases and create one-off

update that works as a nice opportunity to reach out to leads. That’s a great
time to arm your sales team with a handy template that they can then
customize to various prospects, industries or regions as it relates to them.
Another example is if your company is hosting an upcoming event. Give
the sales team a template with information and offers regarding the event
in order to strike up fresh conversations.

Reports/Whitepapers
What they’re all about
Reports and whitepapers are ways to boost your brand’s credibility among
potential customers. Reports can include annual reports, surveys, or
industry benchmark reports. Whitepapers are persuasive reports on a given
topic that address the problem and the solution. For both of these types of
content, they are beneficial in the sales cycle if prospects are seeking proof
of your company’s authority within your industry.
How to make them
For annual reports, compile all of the public information you can share
about your company into a designed report. For this, you can include how
many customers you gained, how much you helped them improve or how
much impact you had on their success, where your customers are located,
and other interesting information like community service hours performed
by your team or pieces of content created throughout the year.
Surveys are great ways to add legitimacy to your brand. To create an
industry survey, you can use Airtable forms to collect data from the
countries of your choice. You can then analyze the data and present it in
a designed format, then share the report with those in your industry. This
is a great sales enablement tool, especially if the report gains notoriety
in your field and among competitors. (Bonus: reports are beneficial to
SEO strategy—particularly if they receive coverage and backlinks from
industry blogs.)

Competitor Analysis
What’s it all about
A competitor analysis is a document that easily shows the differences in
features between your product or service and your main competitors. Arm
your sales team with competitive battlecards so they know how to set your
product apart from the competition, how to assert what you do better, and
be aware of where your competition might have an advantage—it eliminates
surprises, and allows them to confidently guide the conversation. Prepare
your sales team with thoughtful responses to major concerns and handle
objections from customers when they request features that are not yet
available.
How to do it
Create a matrix of the various products in the market, their features, UI,
messaging, and market share - than analyze each of these considerations
against your own product, and keep your sales team knowledgeable on
where you exceed or where there’s room for growth.
