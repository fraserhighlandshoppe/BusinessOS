MISTAKE #1: No headline or title giving a compelling
reason to sign up.
By “compelling reason,” what we mean is you need to be solving a
problem your target audience is struggling with. And it’s not a general
feeling. It needs to be specific. It needs to be what we call a “wakeup-in-the-morning problem.” It’s the first thing they think about when
they wake up in the morning. It’s something with emotion.

MISTAKE #2: Page is too busy and not exclusive.
The opt-in page should have no distractions. Your intention is
to get visitors to enter their contact info (in exchange for a free
valuable gift) and NOTHING ELSE.
No website header, no navigation buttons, no links to read
other articles.
Nothing but the opt-in opportunity!
Don’t be sucked in by the desire for people to know all about
your products and services, about your bio, etc. There will be
time for them to get to know you. But for now, the ONLY action
visitors should be able to take is to opt in.

2

If there are too many distractions, they will…well…get distracted
and may not ever get back to giving you information that will allow
you to keep in touch with them!

MISTAKE #3: Opt-in FORM is not immediately visible.

3

There’s an old newspaper term called “below the fold,” which refers to
the articles on the bottom half of the front page that are not seen when
a newspaper is folded on newsstands. The most important articles are
always placed “above the fold.”
In the online world, “below the fold” means that something is not
immediately visible, that you have to scroll down to see it.
It’s essential that your opt-in box is instantly visible at the top of the
page, no scrolling needed. When we say opt-in box, we mean the
box where people put their name and email. Otherwise, there’s a good
chance it will be missed altogether.

MISTAKE #4: Opt-in form asks for too much
information.
Sometimes you’ll see an opt-in form that asks for your first name,
last name, email, phone number, mailing address, date of birth,
favorite colors, and on and on.

4

This is what often causes what’s known as “form abandonment.”
Someone starts to fill out your opt-in form but, halfway through,
they stop. They leave. They never finish filling it out.
So the ONLY two pieces of information you need to ask for are
First Name and Email Address.
If people are just “meeting” you for the first time, they don’t even
know if they LIKE you yet. So the more personal the information you
ask them for, the more uncomfortable they’re going to feel in giving
you ANYTHING. You have to earn their trust before they’ll be willing
to divulge more.
Sometimes you’ll see forms that ask for cell phone numbers—this
makes it possible for the host of the call to use text message reminders. While the text message reminders will help people to show
up to the call, it does decrease opt-in percentage—especially for
cold traffic.

MISTAKE #5: No images.
We’re visual creatures. The saying, “A picture is worth a
thousand words” is true. We want to SEE what we’re getting. So you want to use images that do that—that show us
what we’re getting.
You can do that by having an image of the gift or product itself, or you can have an aspirational image, one that
shows the emotion that your client is going to feel or what
they want to create.

5

A happy woman, a beach, someone who spends time with
family, a bridge, a landscape. Use images that portray the
feeling of the outcome you are promising. It doesn’t have to
be literal, it just needs to show feeling.

MISTAKE #7: Too much copy.
If you’ve ever thought that writing opt-in pages seems to take
forever—you write and write and HOPE that people will opt in—
then you’re definitely writing too much copy.
You just need the essentials:
•

Why do they need this thing you’re offering in exchange for
their name and email address? How can it help them?

•

You don’t need to go on and on about the struggle or
problem they’re dealing with. Just hit it specifically
and briefly.

•

Then, instead of writing 3 pages about the transformation that’s
possible, just address it as concisely as possible. Sometimes a
title and subtitle of the gift you’re offering are all you need.
