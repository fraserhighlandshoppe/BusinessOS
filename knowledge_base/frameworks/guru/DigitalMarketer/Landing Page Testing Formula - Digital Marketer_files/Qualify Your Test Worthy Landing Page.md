Qualify Your Test Worthy Landing Page
All tests are not created equal!
Before you run a test, makes sure you are running quality tests. There are four main considerations when selecting and qualifying your tests.
Identifying if it’s an offer or structure test.
The lift potential
The ease of test implementation
The KPIs
1. Identifying if it’s an offer or structure test
The first step is to decide whether you are testing your offer or if you are testing the the page structure.
Offer Tests - this would be anything pertaining to what your visitor will get if they convert. Are you providing a checklist or a video? If they are purchasing something, is the price a fair representation of the goods offered? This is the most overlooked step by CROs.
Never assume that the offer is good just because that’s the offer you’ve always used. No amount of structural tweaks can make a bad offer convert!
Structural Tests - These are the standard types of tests you see run by optimization agencies and in CRO case studies.
These types of tests try new copy, images, page layouts, among other types of elements. We’ll discuss more of these elements in detail in section 4.
Don’t start with a structural test, make sure you have a good offer then optimize the page structure.
2. The Lift Potential
Consider the probability that the test you will run will provide a dramatic increase in the desired behavior (opt-ins, purchases, clicks, etc)
Tests with a high lift percentage require a smaller sample size. So on top of having a great immediate impact on your conversions, tests with a high lift potential can also be completed faster then tests with smaller gains.
3. The Ease Of Test Implementation
There are two major considerations here:
A) The ease of running the test.
Running the test requires your testing tech tool to be placed on all measured pages (not just the test page itself). Every test will require these basics:
Code on your site
Goal Links
Test Assets, e.g., copy, Images, etc…
The inability to produce or implement test assets can lead to delays in your test launch. The more complex the needed assets or changes to the website, the longer this period will take.
B) The ease of getting the winning variation live.
If you run a test, but will never have the ability to make the winning variation live --- don't run the test.
Most landing page tests are either smaller scale tweaks or larger radical redesigns. The former isn’t a major change for developers and the latter is likely a split URL test.
4) The KPIs
The more business oriented your KPIs, the higher priority the test. Tests that focus on vanity metrics like clicks or Facebook Likes, are not going to directly impact your business goals. As marketers we love looking at all of our fun marketing metrics, but most business people are only concerned with two things:  Revenue and Costs.
Higher priority KPIs are the KPIs that are directly connected to business goals. This would include revenue per visitor, earnings per click, average order value, and purchases.
