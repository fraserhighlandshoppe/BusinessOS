How to spot a great deal... in 10 seconds or less
Many people get stuck on the analysis of a property – they are afraid to make a mistake!
This is commonly known as analysis paralysis and is very common in left-brained
engineering types of people.
In general, you should act on a deal if you have collected 70% of the information. If you
wait for 90% or more, the deal will probably be gone.
Acting on a deal requires making quick decisions, and making quick decisions requires
knowing your deal criteria up front and taking calculated risks.
So everyone is looking for a great real estate deal. But what exactly is a great deal? Ask
10 different people and you’ll get 10 different answers.
Why is that? Because the answer depends on the needs of the investor. Are they looking
for…
Cash flow from rental properties? And how much cash flow do they expect?
Equity gains from flipping property? How much of a gain?

Now for those of you who don’t know what these words mean, here are some quick
definitions…

Cash Flow
The movement of cash, either positive or negative amounts, through an investment
vehicle. You receive positive cash flow or you pay negative cash flow.
For real estate, the basic formula is:

Total Income – Total Expenses = Cash flow
Equity
The amount of money left over after all liabilities/debts are subtracted from the
market value of an asset/investment. For real estate, the basic formula is:

Total Assets (Market Value of Investment) – Total Liabilities = Equity

Rentals

Remember… for rental property, a good deal is one which generates cash flow. The
property may still be priced under market value and could be a bargain, but it is not a
good deal from a cash flow perspective.

Let’s look at two examples…

Example #1:
Joe finds a duplex listed on the MLS for $150,000
Total monthly rent is $700 per unit = $700 x 2 = $1400 per month
Total annual income = $1400 x 12 = $16,800
Minimum annual income = $150,000 / 10 = $15,000
The total annual income is more than the minimum annual income, therefore, this
is probably a good deal and requires further analysis of income and expenses.

Example #2:
Mary finds a single family home listed on the MLS for $180,000
Total monthly rent is $1200 per month
Total annual income = $1200 x 12 = $14,400
Minimum annual income = $180,000 / 10 = $18,000
The total annual income is less than the minimum annual income, therefore, this
is probably NOT a good deal. You could still perform a further analysis of
income and expenses, but chances are it will not cash flow.

This technique is just a general rule of thumb to help you save time when looking at
deals. There are exceptions to this rule and by using it, you will miss some deals. But if

Flips
The process for analyzing a property for flipping is much easier than for rentals.
However, it does require knowledge of:
•
•

Market value of similar properties in the area
Approximate cost of any repairs or renovations required for the property

Market values can be determined by personally studying the real estate market, or by
asking a realtor for help. Repair or renovation costs can be determined using a cost guide
provided by a property inspector, or by asking a contractor for an estimate.
Here is an example of a free cost guide provided by a property inspection company I use:
http://www.propertyinspection.ca/download/cost_guide.html

Here’s a quick 5-step process you can use to quickly determine if a property is probably
a good deal for flipping. You can either perform this calculation in your head or with a
calculator in 10 seconds or less…
1. Determine the purchase price of the property
2. Determine the cost of any repairs required for the property
3. Add the purchase price and cost of repairs to determine the After Repaired Value
(ARV)
4. Determine the market value of the property if the property was in good condition
5. Determine the price discount = After Repaired Value / Market Value * 100
‘After Repaired Value’ means the value of the property after all renovations are
complete, and the property is ready for sale.
If the price discount is:
•
•

81% to 100% of the property market value, it probably IS NOT a good deal
80% or less of the property market value, it probably IS a good deal

Remember… for flip property, a good deal is one which generates equity gains. The
property may still generate cash flow and could be a bargain as a rental, but it is not a
good deal from an equity gain perspective.

How to use other people’s money to buy property
Everyone runs out of cash or credit eventually. There are no exceptions – even Donald
Trump has limited cash on hand. When resources are depleted, an individual can wait to
accumulate more cash and credit to buy additional investments, or they can speed things
up by using…

Other people’s money (OPM)
Other people’s credit (OPC)
Does that mean that people just hand you the cash to invest? Not really. Maybe your

There are 3 main ways to use other people’s money:

Joint ventures
Private loans
Private mortgages

Joint Ventures
Dictionary.com defines it as "A business undertaken by two or more individuals or
companies in an effort to share risk and use differences in expertise". Large corporations
use joint ventures all the time to expand the reach of their business and grow their
revenue.

I have expanded this definition for real estate investing…
"A business undertaken by two or more individuals or companies in an effort to
share risk, use resources, time, and differences in expertise for a common goal"

A real estate joint venture (also called syndication) is usually accomplished by multiple
investors (either people or corporations) coming together to buy an investment. Be aware
that federal or provincial government regulations may limit the number of investors that
can enter into a joint venture.

Private Loans
A relatively simple way to use other people’s money is to simply borrow it. That can
sometimes be easier said than done.
Some people think borrowing from friends and family, at no interest or low interest, is
the best way to go. However, you could easily lose your friends and alienate your family
if you can’t repay the money.

Others think borrowing from strangers is a better approach. However, most strangers will
not loan money to people they don’t know, especially the large sums required for real
estate, unless they have a written agreement along with some sort of security for their
money. They will also usually require interest to be paid on the loan.

The agreement usually includes:
•
•
•
•

The total amount loaned
The interest rate charged
The term of the loan (length of time)
Any security required (e.g. ownership of a car, boat, locked-in GIC, etc.)

When the security required is personal property, such as a car or boat, the lender may
require the agreement be registered with the local courthouse or other personal property
registry (may vary by province). This protects the lender and allows them to repossess
the personal property if payment is not made.

The biggest problem with private loans occurs when you try to obtain financing for the
main mortgage on the property you want to buy. All banks require you disclose exactly
where your down payment money is coming from. If you have borrowed the funds, the
banks will usually want to know the terms and conditions of the loan, and it will count
against you when you try to qualify for the mortgage.
However, some lenders may allow this, so it’s always best to talk with your banker or
mortgage broker to see what is possible.
Investing secret – Banks will accept gifts from family members as proof of down
payment. Specifically, a letter written from your parents stating they will give you
money should be sufficient proof of down payment.

Private Mortgages
Another way to come up with the extra money to buy property is to obtain a second
mortgage. So what’s a mortgage and how does a second mortgage work?

When you apply for financing on a property, the bank’s loan will appear as the first
mortgage on the title of the property. A mortgage is similar to a loan, but there are a few
subtle differences…
•
•

A mortgage is secured against a piece of real estate (loans are not)
Mortgage payments are usually a changing blend of interest and principal
repayment (loans are usually an unchanging blend of interest and principal
repayment)

A second mortgage is any mortgage that is registered on the title of the property after
the first mortgage.

Very few banks offer second mortgages – they are usually offered by private investors,
typically through mortgage brokers. The disadvantage to using these types of investors is
that they usually have very strict lending requirements, sometimes as strict as normal
banks. I don’t like that.

My favorite way to obtain a second mortgage is to find my own private investor who
has the cash. And get this… instead of the investor requiring cash in their bank account,
I’ve figured out how to use their RRSP money instead. This is called an RRSP mortgage.

Think about that for a minute.
How many billions of dollars are sitting in RRSPs? Could you use some of that money to
invest in property? Oh, and I forgot to mention… it also applies to RRIFs and LIRAs as
well. Don’t worry if you don’t know what those are, just know that they are an option.
You must be wondering… “Is this legal?”
Yes, it’s absolutely 100% legal. Even Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) knows about
them.
The best part about these types of mortgages is that they can be MUCH more flexible
than any other bank or private investor.

How buying with no money down can be the worst way to
invest… if you do it the wrong way
Almost everyone has heard about buying properties with nothing down. Many people
don’t believe it’s possible, but it can happen. I know because I’ve done it.
However, if it’s done the wrong way, it can quickly become the worst thing you’ve ever
done.
How bad could it be if none of your own money is invested?
In most cases, the secret to no money down deals is not that the property seller gets no
money at closing… it’s that they don’t get any of YOUR money.

And that means the money has to come from somewhere else. Most of the time, it comes
in the form of a personal loan or special type of mortgage on the property. There are
other creative ways to do it, but these are the most common.
The problem with these methods is that:
•
•

The money must eventually be paid back
Interest is usually charged on the borrowed funds, often with monthly payments

And that requires you are good at managing money and especially cash flow.

Why is that a problem for many people?
People usually want nothing down deals because they have no money

If they have no money, it’s usually because they are bad at money management and
especially at managing cash flow. Can you see the problem?

Nothing down investing requires good money and cash flow management skills,
But most people who invest nothing down don’t have these skills

There are a lot of things that can go wrong in real estate investing, and if you aren’t
prepared or experienced in how to handle them, or if you don’t have the cash flow to
handle the hard times, these deals can very quickly eat you alive financially.

Why can cash flow be a problem? Nothing down deals are usually highly leveraged.
This means that there is a large amount of loans or mortgaging financing on the property
compared to the amount of cash invested. As a result, these deals don’t produce any
positive cash flow.

What are some of the things that can go wrong?
•
•
•
•
•
•
•
•

Interest rates can go up on variable mortgages
Interest rates can go up just when a fixed mortgage comes due for renewal
Tenants can vandalize your apartment, requiring major repairs
Tenants can stop paying rent, forcing you to evict them (this can easily take 1-2
months, during which time you collect no money from them)
The roof, furnace, or one of a hundred other items can break down, requiring cash
to pay for replacement
Tenants can call the city on you for repairs that are required and have a bylaw
enforcement officer place work orders on your property until the repairs are
complete
Fire department can inspect your property and force you to do expensive fire
retrofit upgrades
Property manager can go bankrupt and take your rent (it happened to us)

Any of these situations can very easily force you to run out of cash, and as a result, be
forced to sell the property at a loss or worse, go bankrupt. And if you think it can’t
happen to you, think again. A lot of people have lost everything they owned because
they didn’t know about the risks of leverage and how to reduce them.
Remember these 3 rules about nothing down investing….
•
•
•

Learn how to manage money and cash flow, and watch your cash flow like a hawk
Have a plan to pay out any extra loans or mortgage financing when they come due
Have extra cash in the bank or credit to handle the hard times (they will come)

If you’re going to invest in nothing down deals, you must learn money management and
cash flow management skills. It’s a requirement!
These are skills that can be learned, just like any other skill. However, they are not taught
in our school system and even college and university programs don’t always teach them
properly.
Get started today… Buy some business and financial accounting books and start learning.
