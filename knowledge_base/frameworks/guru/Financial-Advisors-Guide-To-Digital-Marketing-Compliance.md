HOW TO HANDLE THE LINKEDIN ENDORSEMENT FEATURE
LinkedIn’s Endorsement feature on an advisor’s profile page is under the control of the advisor. In addition, the endorsement
feature provides only positive feedback. With these factors in mind, and because LinkedIn members have the discretion to
accept or deny any endorsement, many compliance experts recommend that advisors turn off this feature.

ACTION THIS IF COMPLIANCE HAS A PROBLEM:
If you and your compliance officer determine that turning off the endorsements feature is a good idea, log in to LinkedIn and
click “Edit Profile” then “Edit Skills and Expertise.” If you already have endorsements on your LinkedIn profile, rather than
deleting them, it might be best to simply “Hide” them (who knows – you might not be with the same firm in the future and
could potentially show them at some point).

WHAT THE SEC ACTUALLY SAYS ABOUT ADVERTISING
The SEC Advertising Rule has a pretty broad definition of what constitutes
an “advertisement”.
RULE 206(4)-1
Rule 206(4)-1 under the Advisers Act prohibits SEC-registered
investment advisers from using any advertisement that contains any
untrue statement of material fact, or that is otherwise misleading.
The rule broadly defines “advertisement” to include any notice,
circular, letter, or other written communication addressed to more
than one person, or any notice or other announcement in any
publication or by radio or television, which offers any investment
advisory service.
Also, the SEC Advertising Rule specifically prohibits an investment
adviser from publishing, circulating or distributing any advertisement
that:
Refers to any testimonial concerning the investment adviser or any
advice, analysis, report, or other service rendered by such investment
adviser

THE ADVERTISING BACK DOOR THE FEDS LEFT WIDE OPEN FOR YOU!
If you look at the enormous amounts of rules and regulation regarding advertising... the majority of it is directly linked to
buying/selling securities and “performance advertising”.
The SEC and FINRA want to protect the consumer from fraudulent or misleading claims about a firm’s performance.
However... if you look a little bit closer at the supplementary material for FINRA Rule 2111: Suitability, you’ll discover
an open door.
F I N R A R U L E 2 1 1 1 : S U I TA B I L I T Y :
FINRA Rule 2111: Suitability: The phrase “investment strategy involving a security or securities” used in this Rule is to be
interpreted broadly and would include, among other things, an explicit recommendation to hold a security or securities.
However, the following communications are excluded from the coverage of Rule 2111 as long as they do not include (standing
alone or in combination with other communications) a recommendation of a particular security or securities:

HOW TO ADVERTISE WITHOUT COMPLIANCE PROBLEMS
From what you have read so far, you might be frustrated by the rules and regulations limiting the way you advertise. The
truth is advertising for you can be easy and profitable when done the right way.
Most advisors focus on the what and how in their business, meaning our products, performance, or what their process
is like. This creates a problem because your market will not respond to that and the Feds frown upon advertising based
performance.
When you try to talk about yourself, you will face some of the strictest restrictions and regulations you can imagine.
The obvious tactics you would use to talk about your services would be:
•

Testimonials (which you can’t do)

•

Product advertising (Lots of rules around this)

•

Performance of your services (Even more rules around this)

Instead, you need to look at the problems your potential clients are facing and how you help solve those problems. Do this
effectively and immediately the advertising opportunities open up for you.
