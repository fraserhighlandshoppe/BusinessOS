The eighth and final step in the Customer Journey is to take your
advocates—those customers who will say nice things about your
company, when asked—and turn them into promoters.
The difference between an advocate and a promoter is that a promoter
will actively promote your brand.
Instead of waiting to be asked about your company, a promoter will

overstated. Remember that we live in a world where consumers are less
and less trustful of what companies say. People today put far more trust
in third-party reviews and testimonials. And that means that the more
promoters you can get for your business, the more credibility you will
create --- and the faster your company can grow.

The business world is in violent agreement that Sales and Marketing

make buying decisions. And yet, so few companies have been able

As with most things in business, execution comes down to three steps:
Set a goal
Position people to meet that goal
Execute

STEP

SET A GOAL

In 2001, Jim Collins released his best-selling book, Good to Great,
about how mediocre companies can transform into market leaders with
virtually unstoppable momentum. The book studied the performance
of 1,435 companies over 40 years and found that the companies
that went from good to great all leveraged something Jim calls The
Flywheel Effect.
Here’s an excerpt from Good to Great (Harper Collins, 2001):

Picture a huge, heavy flywheel—a massive metal disk mounted horizontally
on an axle, about 30 feet in diameter, 2 feet thick, and weighing about
5,000 pounds. Now imagine that your task is to get the flywheel rotating on
the axle as fast and long as possible.
Pushing with great effort, you get the flywheel to inch forward, moving
almost imperceptibly at first. You keep pushing and, after two or three
hours of persistent effort, you get the flywheel to complete one entire turn.
You keep pushing, and the flywheel begins to move a bit faster, and with
continued great effort, you move it around a second rotation. You keep
pushing in a consistent direction. Three turns ... four ... five ... six ... the
flywheel builds up speed ... seven ... eight ... you keep pushing ... nine ...
ten ... it builds momentum ... eleven ... twelve ... moving faster with each
turn … twenty … thirty … fifty … a hundred.
Then, at some point—breakthrough! The momentum of the thing kicks in
your favor, hurling the flywheel forward, turn after turn ... whoosh! ...
its own heavy weight working for you. You’re pushing no harder than
during the first rotation, but the flywheel goes faster and faster. Each
turn of the flywheel builds upon work done earlier, compounding your
investment of effort. A thousand times faster, then ten thousand, then
a hundred thousand. The huge heavy disk flies forward, with almost
unstoppable momentum.
- Jim Collins, Good to Great

The question Jim Collins poses in this landmark book is “What was the
one big push that caused things to go so fast?” This is, of course, the
wrong question to ask. There is no single push that moves the flywheel.
Instead, as Jim Collins points out, it is the “overall accumulation of
effort applied in a consistent direction.”
Are your Sales and Marketing teams pushing in the same direction?
Most Sales and Marketing teams are driven by two different goals:
creating demand (Marketing) and fulfilling on existing demand (Sales).
Organizations stumble when marketing’s only concern is producing
leads, with no accountability for actual sales made. While, at the same
time the Sales team has no incentive to work toward producing new and
better leads. As long as these two departments have different goals,
they will push the flywheel in different directions or not at all.
The first critical step in integrating your Sales and Marketing
departments is to give them a common direction to push the flywheel.
That common goal is growth.
So, the right question to ask is, “How do we get everyone pushing the
flywheel in the direction of growth?”
Remember that, today, the customer has more control over the
marketing and sales process than ever before. To achieve growth
your teams must by synchronized with the new way your customer is
researching and making purchases.

STEP

2
POSITION PEOPLE TO
MEET A GOAL

Research shows that a lack of alignment between sales and marketing
hurts company performance and some companies are taking drastic
action. Companies such as Coca-Cola, Campbell’s Soup and FedEx
now have a Chief Revenue Officer (CRO) whose responsibility is
achieving the revenue goals of the company. This new C-suite level
position often has control over the Marketing, Sales and Customer
Service departments.
For most companies, moving marketing, sales and customer service
under a central authority is a drastic reorganization. If that’s not a
possibility in your company, create a Growth Committee. This way
you can still preserve your existing organizational structure, while still
gaining the benefit of Sales and Marketing alignment.
The important thing is that you recognize the importance of centralizing
the responsibility of growth under a single team or committee. Your
“Growth Team” (or Growth Committee) should be composed of four
sub-teams:

Acquisition Team
Primary Responsibilities: Aware, Subscribe, Convert
Description: Your Acquisition team is responsible for generating
leads. Traditionally, you would call this “Marketing”; but as part of
your Growth Team, their work will become much more integrated
into the greater effort of progressing your prospects through the
Customer Journey. As a result, they will generate much more
qualified leads that result in more customers and revenue.

Content Team
Primary Responsibilities: Engage, Subscribe
Description: Your Content team includes anyone who produces
content that is used to engage prospects, generate leads, and
close sales. Traditionally, these functions would be performed by
a combination of your Editorial Department, Public Relations, or
your Social Media team.

Monetization Team
Primary Responsibilities: Ascend
Description: Your Monetization team is responsible for
monetizing the leads generated by your Acquisition team. You
would normally call this your Sales department; but as part
of your Growth Team, sales become easier and more organic
in nature. Because you have already built up value with your
prospects by progressing them through the first half of the
Customer Journey, they will be much more ready to buy.

Success Team
Primary Responsibilities: Excite, Advocate, Promote
Description: Your Success Team is responsible for making
sure your new customers have a fantastic experience with your
brand. This includes encouraging them to consume or use your
product or service, onboarding new customers, and training your
customers how to get value from your products or services.
While each sub-team has primary responsibilities, every member
of the Growth Team is responsible for achieving the common
goal: growth.

To execute properly your teams must be following the same strategy,
using the same tactics, and speaking the same language. Doing this is the
only way to make sure that everyone’s efforts come together cohesively to
generate a result that’s greater than the sum of its parts.
This is no small task in today’s modern workplace, in which each of your
employees has a unique history that includes different experiences,
perspectives, and training.
Imagine that heavy flywheel again, except this time everyone is pushing
and pulling the flywheel in different directions. And worse, imagine some
team members are pushing different flywheels entirely. Effort is expended.
Money is spent. Time is consumed. And yet the flywheel doesn’t move.
It sounds farfetched, but this is essentially what is happening in many
companies today.
What you need, of course, is a playbook. A shared set of strategy,
tactics and language that everyone can learn, agree on, and execute to
achieve growth.
