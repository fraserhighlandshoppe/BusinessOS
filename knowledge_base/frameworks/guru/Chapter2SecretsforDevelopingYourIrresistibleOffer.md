Chapter 2: Secrets for Developing Your Irresistible Offer
“If you help enough other people get what they want, you can get everything in life
that​ you want.” —Zig Ziglar.
An irresistible offer is one perfectly matched to your prospects’ wants, needs, and desires.
You cannot craft your irresistible offer without first getting deep into your prospects’
minds, connecting with their pain and frustration as covered in the previous chapter. Once
you have discovered exactly what they really want, then you can develop your irresistible
offer around that.
Often when people put together an offer of products or services, they think about what they
want to sell as opposed to what the prospect actually wants to buy. The winning formula is
to find a hot target market, find out what that target market wants, and then give them
what they want. That formula may sound really simple and it is, but it is profound. The
worst thing that you can do is to develop an offer and then try to find an audience to sell it
to.
When most people craft a sales presentation, they start at the beginning and work their
way through to the end, the close. You should do the opposite, working backwards
whenever you're developing any sales presentation. You can't develop a great presentation
until you know specifically what it is that you're selling. If you have a truly powerful offer
that's matched to your prospect's wants, needs, and desires, you could actually do a
mediocre sales presentation and still get pretty darn good results. On the other hand, if you
have an offer that is weak, is not perfectly matched to what your prospect wants, even a
perfect sales presentation won’t get good results.
Using the Hierarchy of Persuasion
In the previous chapter, you learned that to sell your prospects what they truly want, you
must not only get deep within their minds to find out what they want but also deliver the
transformation that they desire. We explained the hierarchy of persuasion, and now will
discuss using it in developing an offer. To review: At the lowest rung are the features,
specific things about the product or service. On the next level up are benefits—what the
feature does for your prospect. If you put all the benefits together, you can reach the next
highest level, the outcomes that someone is going to achieve. Most people who are selling
never actually get to that level, but you are going to take your persuasion up to the top level
of offering your prospects transformation.

Let's use an example from a financial advisor. A feature would be the advisor providing
their prospective clients access to some quality financial product. The benefit is that it’s a
safe place to put their money and watch it grow. The outcome is that they're going to be
able to retire without worrying about money. That’s simple so far. But you can't really craft
an irresistible offer unless you know what the transformation is. The transformation for
the advisor’s prospects is that they'll be able to live their golden age the way they want.
They'll be able to take trips to see the world or visit their far-flung family members. That’s
what I mean when I say the transformation is emotional and personal.
A reaction I sometimes get when I say this is, "Well, that's true if you're selling business to
consumer, but if you're selling business to business, the transformation isn't emotional and
personal." That’s a big misnomer. You're never really selling business to business. You're
selling to a person or a group of people in a business, and they have two agendas. Their first
agenda is obviously to do something positive for the business. But their second agenda is to
do something positive for themselves, for example to look good in the eyes of the boss.
Whatever and wherever you are selling, the hierarchy of persuasion is always there. You've
got to know what the desired transformation is to craft an irresistible offer to meet that
desire.
Developing Your Offer
To begin putting together an irresistible offer, you should create several sheets, either on
paper or in your computer word processing program. The first sheet should be titled
“Features” and list all of the features of the product that you're selling—in excruciating
detail. If you have an informational product with a manual, you specify that it comes in a
three-ring binder, runs 168 pages, double-sided and in color. If you're an estate-planning
attorney and your presentation is “selling” a free initial consultation, write that the feature
is a sixty-minute consultation at your office at x-y-z address. In other words, spell out every
detail of every feature of your product or service. You’ll understand the method to this
madness as we go along.
Free Offers
Some people mistakenly think that when they're offering something for free they
don't have to sell as hard as they do if they are selling an actual product or service.
Just because something is free doesn't mean that people are going to take advantage
of it. These days there is a proliferation of information available on demand. Let’s
say you’re a financial advisor, offering a free consultation. Your prospects may have
received a multitude of similar offers, and they know you're going to try to sell them
something. So, you have to construct your offer and sell it just as hard as you would
if you were actually selling a product for a fee.

Elements of the Deal
The next sheet is called the deal sheet. It’s where you're going to take all of the features and
benefits and put them into the actual deal. While the actual deal depends on what you are
selling, there are some consistent elements you must include.
One of the most important elements is the guarantee. In any transaction, someone is
assuming more of the risk than the other person. For you to make a lot of sales and for you
to create a buying frenzy, you have to assume the risk. You can't have your prospect
assume the risk. So, you want to do some form of what is called risk reversal or having a
guarantee.
Yes, YOU Can Offer a Guarantee
A lot of entrepreneurs or professionals say, "I can't offer a guarantee because I'm a
(fill in the blank). For example, they say, “I'm a financial advisor, and I can't
guarantee that you're going to get a fifteen percent return on your investments.” Or
they say, “I'm a doctor, and I can't guarantee that I'm going to cure you.” That’s true,
but there's always something to guarantee. In offering a free initial consultation, you
can guarantee that if they don't think it was worth their time, you'll donate some
amount of money to their favorite charity. So there’s a way to guarantee even a free
consultation.
Here are a few different types of guarantees:
Money-back guarantee:​ If the buyer is not completely satisfied in some specified time
period, say ninety days, you will give the money back. That is a standard, unconditional
satisfaction guarantee.
Results-based guarantee:​ If the buyer performs the specified steps according to
instructions, you guarantee he or she will get a certain result. So, if you buy my course for
$997, I guarantee that if you do all of the steps, you'll make at least twenty times that
amount within the first ninety days or I'll give you your money back. The
performance-based or results-based guarantee is conditional.
Double guarantee:​ Combining a conditional guarantee with an unconditional guarantee.
For example: Buy my program, and if you are not completely satisfied with your results
after sixty days, return it for a full refund, no questions asked. If you keep the program past
sixty days, you’ll still have one full year to try it out, and if you can show me that you've

done x, y, and z, and did not get the promised results, I'll still refund all of your money.
That type of double guarantee is powerful. The longer the guarantee, the less refunds you
have. So, if you give buyers ten days to decide whether they're going to keep your product,
that decision is constantly on their minds. If you give them a longer guarantee, they don't
feel the pressure of having to do everything and get a result that quickly. The longer
guarantee actually cuts down on refunds.
The plus guarantee:​ You say not only will you give the dissatisfied buyers their money
back, but will give them something additional. For example, some financial advisors charge
for an initial consultation. Suppose they are developing a financial plan for the client in that
session and charging $500. They say, “If you can look me in the eyes at the end of our
session and tell me that it wasn't worth every single penny, whether you decide to move
ahead with me or not, I will give you not only the $500 back, but I'll also donate an
additional $500 to your favorite charity.”
Another common plus guarantee for someone who's selling an information product that
includes a bonus offer is to tell the buyer, “Keep the program for sixty days, and if at the
end of sixty days, if it isn't for you, send it back to me, but keep the bonuses as my gift to
you for just trying out the product.”
I have used the plus guarantee at my seminars. I have told those attending that if they
stayed for the whole session and could come up to me afterward, look me in the eye and
say it wasn't worth way more than the time and money they invested, I would write them a
refund check right there and pay an additional $500 in documented travel expenses.
Not only do guarantees reverse the risk from the buyer to the seller, guarantees have
another very powerful hidden benefit. They show confidence. Remember, a great part of
selling is the transference of emotion. You are projecting how you feel about your product
or service, your confidence in its being able to produce the results, the outcomes, and the
transformation that your prospect desires. By offering a really powerful guarantee, you're
proving that you are confident in what you have to offer.
Is anyone actually going to take you up on your guarantee? Well, here is the truth. Yes. If
you are the financial advisor guaranteeing a $500 refund and an additional $500 to charity,
you might be out $1,000. Some people will take you up on a guarantee. But most people are
inherently honest, and if you do your job well and deliver on what you promise, the
percentage is so small in relation to the resulting increase in sales that it doesn't matter.
Your guarantee is an important part of your irresistible offer, so write down what your
guarantee is on your deal sheet. If you have any uncertainty about the ethical, legal, or
financial implications of making what could be a contractually binding promise to others,
always seek the advice of professional advisors, such as your attorney and accountant.

Your Pricing Structure
There are many different pricing formulas. I'm going to give you one of my favorites as a
starting point.
A key to creating an irresistible offer is to build up the value of what you have to offer, a
point we’ll cover in more detail in Chapter X. Referring to the sheets on which you have
written in detail the features and benefits of your product or service, assign each one a
value. So, for example, you might value each recorded audio lesson you are selling at $197.
How do you determine that kind of value? Well, value is determined by what you say and
can legitimately justify. If your online course has six hour-long modules, you could say that
if your prospects were to hire you to do private consultation walking them each through
the same steps personally, it would cost them $1,000 an hour, so those six modules have a
real world value of $6,000. I am comparing apples to oranges, but it sounds logical enough
to justify the value for our purpose here.
Once you have added up the values you have assigned to all of the components of your
offer, you've got a big price that you've justified. Then you can tell your prospect, "But
obviously I'm not going to charge you $6,000 for this program." Then you drop it down to
the normal retail price: “If you were to go on my website and invest in this course, it would
cost you $2,997.” Then you say, "But since you've come to this seminar today, if you take
advantage of this right now, you won't pay $2,997. You can get everything for just $997."
You have built up the value and justified the value. You've dropped the price to the retail
price and then you've dropped it one more time to the actual price that you're selling it for.
Your prospect is thinking, "Oh man, $6,000. Well, you know what? $2,997, that seems fair.
Oh my goodness, $997, I've got to get it." If you just came right out of the gate and said it
was $997, you're not going to create that same emotional reaction. The psychology is
completely different. You can adapt the same formula if you're a financial advisor offering a
free, two-hour consultation, starting off saying you normally charge $1,000 an hour. So, the
value of the consultation is $2,000, but if you're one of the first four people at the luncheon
presentation to go to the back of the room now and request a consultation, you'll get it
absolutely free.
Even if you're giving away something for free, you've got to give it a value. If you don't put a
value on it, then your prospect doesn't put a value on it.

Give Them a Reason to Act Now
Creating scarcity is another part of developing an irresistible offer. If you don't give a
reason for your prospects to take action now, most people will put off a decision. You’ve
just seen one technique to provide a reason for action: the financial advisor who said a
$2,000 consultation would be free for the first four audience members who requested it. In
a webinar you can simply say, "I only allot ten appointments a month to do these free
consultations because I'm busy working with clients. Once those ten are up, you're going to
have to wait until the next month. So, to take advantage of this offer and be among the first
ten, make sure you fill out the application form immediately."
A time limit is another way to prompt prospects to act. Let's say you're doing a webinar to
sell a product online. “When you order before the countdown clock hits zero on the order
form,” you say, “you're going to get the special price and you're going to get all of these
bonuses.” Your online audience can see the seconds ticking away on the countdown clock,
which adds a sense of urgency to take advantage of the opportunity or miss out.
People are more motivated by fear of missing out on something than they are by actually
gaining something. Using that psychology can help you create an irresistible offer.
Always Offer Bonuses
An offer of bonuses will move a sale forward no matter what business or profession you're
in. There are two types of bonuses you need to create and offer in your presentation:
Core bonuses​ always come with your product or service regardless of when or where
someone buys it. ​Fast-action bonuses​ are like the examples above that went to the first
four to request a consultation or the buyers who beat the online countdown clock.
The fast-action bonuses add scarcity because they are only available to a limited number of
people or for a limited time. One way I have done this in presentations is to describe a basic
and a deluxe version of my offer, with the deluxe option being far more expensive. At the
end of my presentation, I say, "Hey, if you're one of the first 17 people to take advantage of
this opportunity, you're going to get the deluxe package for the price of the basic package."
Everyone wants the deluxe option, and nobody wants to miss out on a great deal, so they’ll
run to the back of the room to order, or click the buy-now button online if it’s a webinar.
Some of the best bonuses, regardless of the business that you're in, are information-based
bonuses, which are perceived as having a high value relative to what they cost you. You can
offer extra online training. In any business or profession, you can offer a free copy of a
report you have written or an informative audio or video recording. For example: "When
you come into my office, I'm going to give you a copy of my course on how to plan out your
