How to Use This Book
In the months running up to the COVID-19 crisis, many of my friends and colleagues had known that I
was planning to write a book on growing and scaling a business. A big part of my advice would involve
taking a pause and strategically planning out your approach so that you could weather any storm and
emerge stronger than ever before (see Chapters 1-4). But with the advent of the crisis, I knew I had to do
the opposite and move as quickly as possible. Thousands of my clients, friends, and colleagues were
asking me on a daily basis for help, guidance, and advice. I decided to write this book to help people
because I knew it couldn’t wait.
As a result, my team and I went into overdrive. During a three-week sprint, more than 12 people rushed
to assemble this book. We hired an editor to make sure the results were readable and shipped it as
quickly as possible. As a result, if you’re looking for Bill Shakespeare, this book ain’t it. I apologize in
advance for any typos and rough passages, but in such a critical situation, I didn’t want the perfect to
become the enemy of the useful. I am confident this book contains a valuable blueprint for dealing with

any crisis or emergency, regardless of its literary qualities (or lack thereof). Of course, not every
business is facing the same emergency. Some of you have walked into this with plenty of cash
reserves and a solid grasp of your mission, vision, and values. Others have sailed into the crisis
unprepared.

When I encounter an emergency or crisis, the first thing I do is stop, assess the immediate situation,
and then react accordingly. Two things are in your favor: your ability to control your mindset and
time. For me, when the preverbial crap hits the fan, I try and slow both of those things down. My
ability to manage my thinking and my reactions is the best advantage I have. My thought process goes
like this:
Am I okay? ​This is a personal question. In an emergency or crisis, you need to know what your
personal situation is. I’m no good to anyone else if I’m not okay and in control of my mindset
and reactions.
Is my family okay? ​This question is similar to the first. You need to reassure yourself in a scary
situation that your family is safe. If you don’t have this foundation, nothing else will matter.
Are my friends okay? ​Checking in on those you care about will bring comfort. It will also give
you clarity on what, if anything, you can do to help.
Are those that I’m responsible for okay?​ Make sure the people who depend on you are
okay. For example, your employees, customers, vendors, etc.
How much time do I have? ​This is the most critical question for your business. If your
company is going to fail tomorrow, there probably is not much you can do. However, if you can
continue operations anywhere from a day, week, month, or a year (albeit with some drastic
changes), relax. You have the time to follow the lessons in this book to not only save, but also
strengthen your business, turning crisis into opportunity.
Do I know a trusted resource? ​Whenever anything is seriously wrong, our first instinct is to
turn to our friends and family and ask what to do. This is a bad idea. Instead, you need a trusted
resource who has been through, and succeeded, in circumstances similar to the ones you are
facing. The good news is that you have turned to this book, which reflects the experience of
people who have been here before. By following the advice in this book, you should be able to
stabilize your situation and prepare yourself for recovery.

If you have sufficient time, I strongly suggest starting at the beginning of this book and working through
all the chapters, chronologically, from beginning to end. In this way, you will establish the foundational
principles you need to reset your business and prepare it correctly for what’s next. These principles
include everything from defining your goals, understanding your mission,

vision, values, and determining the Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) that you need to measure your
efforts as you go along.
Doing all of these things will likely set your business up for outsized returns during recovery. The reason
being, while you are putting in the hard work of getting your business back on the right track, your
competitors, likely, are not. They will emerge from the crisis the same as they went into it, but in an even
weaker state. You will emerge from it as a much stronger business.
However, if you determine that you simply do not have time for a full reset, you should skip ahead to
Chapter 8. That’s where the rubber meets the road and you start taking decisive action to cut costs and
align your team around a common purpose. Then, once you have your entire business in a life raft
headed for shore, go back and read the rest from the beginning.
This book reflects several lifetimes of experience of starting, growing and turning businesses around:
mine, my partners’, my customers’, my mentors’, and that of the many trusted advisors I’ve met in the
course of my career. Though it may not be the most eloquent book you’ve ever read on business, if you
follow the lessons and suggestions it makes, you should be able to navigate successfully through the
current crisis.
If you have any questions or specific issues you’d like me to address in future books or articles, please
don’t hesitate to reach out to me at www.cardoneventures.com/response or email me
brandon@cardoneventures.com.

CHAPTER 1
Even when it seems like the best of times, an emergency or crisis can quickly overtake your
business. When it does, how you respond in that moment will define you as a leader. Your
reactions and the results they engender will either move you closer to your goals or drive you
away, possibly permanently. Business ownership is something many people say they want, but in
reality, being a business owner tests you at different times and at all levels to find out if you
really have the fortitude to stay in the game at all costs. In good times, people never seem to
recognize that business owners only get capital distributions when things are going well, and
there is money left over. But when a company isn’t doing well and there is no money to spare,
business owners pay in capital contributions.
This is the entry price to business ownership. How you encounter an emergency or crisis is what
separates an entrepreneur with a true long-term commitment to build a business from the one
who is just happy to be his or her own boss. Until the person is staring into the abyss of failure, it
is difficult to know just how committed they are. At that point, their actions will dictate the
ultimate results.
In my experience, what holds entrepreneurs back from growing their businesses is the fear of
outpacing their experience and ability, even in the best of times. As a result, it is easy to
understand why crises and emergencies cause stress, fear, and anxiety. People become paralyzed
trying to make safe, sound, value-centric decisions. Especially when it comes to people, strategy,
and processes, it is almost impossible to make the correct decisions at a speed that protects the
business today, while preserving long-term opportunities. People become overwhelmed and
default to a fight, flight, or freeze response.
At this point the old adage “the bigger you are, the harder you fall” rears its ugly head. People
get so caught up in what might happen if they make a poor choice that they become paralyzed
in their decision-making.
Unfortunately, they need to make the best decisions, acting decisively and executing efficiently,
to secure a lifeline to success. But the less prepared they are before they need to react,

the less they understand the health indicators of their businesses, the more likely damage will
occur. But if you have the experience and knowledge to know and understand the right business
health indicators, you'll find yourself in a very different place.
The purpose of this book is to give you the confidence to act using data, knowledge, and
experience. It will guide you step-by-step on how to make the best decisions, act decisively, and
execute intentionally to protect both short- and long-term value of your business in an
emergency or crisis.
Emergency: ​A serious, unexpected, and often dangerous situation requiring immediate action.
Crisis: ​A time of intense difficulty, trouble, and danger.
For some business owners, emergencies happen every day. They are the result of poor planning,
bad advice, inexperience, or circumstances that could have been avoided. That said, when you
have a macro disruption, like the recent global pandemic, everyone is impacted across the
board. How business owners, governments, and people react to this will define many futures.
Based on my experience, however, one thing is for sure: you can’t always control what’s going
on around you, but you can control how you react and respond. Learning how to control the
controllable is what this book is all about.
It’s important to have context and contrast whenever you initiate change, whether you’re moving
towards an opportunity or away from a painful experience. The more you can isolate and define
what you want to move towards, the more profound and correct your decisions and actions will
be.
Unfortunately, when you're in a state of emergency, your judgment can easily get clouded due to
fear, anxiety, stress, and frustration. If that’s the case, you should stop what you're doing. Take a
deep breath, read this book, and follow the processes it outlines.
To be clear, even the best-run organizations and best-intentioned business owners struggle
during major downturns. I have spent my career building companies and helping businesses of
all sizes grow, scale, merge, integrate, and exit. I’ve also guided them through turnarounds.
When a business is in crisis, the response needs to take into consideration the impact on both
short- and long-term value, not only for the business owners, but also for their teams and
constituents.
The purpose of this book is to give insight, assistance, and guidance so that you can make the
best decisions, take the most impactful actions, and get the best short- and long-term results. My
goal is to help business leaders understand how they should be thinking about decision making
and how to structure and execute on those decisions with maximum impact, while preserving
the human connection and maintaining their emotional state of well-being.

With decision making, the more you can add context and contrast to the equation, the more
logic you can bring into your thought process, the more you can react appropriately. To
understand what I mean, let’s define these terms:
Context: ​The circumstances that form the setting for an event, statement, or idea, and the terms

Contrast: ​The state of being strikingly different from something else in juxtaposition or close

Keep in mind that making any decision in a state of fear or shock – or making no decisions at all
— can create negative momentum that drives you even further into a state of emergency. Worse
still, reacting in the wrong way to short-term problems can drive you into the abyss and move
you even further away from your long-term goals.
It is important when confronted with a business emergency to pause for a moment, ask the right
questions, and solicit information from trusted resources. Move as fast as you can to assimilate
data in order to gain as much context as possible. Until you find data points that you can rely on
to confirm or confront your present thinking, pause on making any big decisions. Once you have
the appropriate data and knowledge, test your decisions by creating contrasting scenarios to fully
think through the steps you’re taking. This book is designed to give you insight into how to do
this, along with helpful strategies from my past experiences.
Remember, the quality of your questions will always determine the value of your results.
Stepping back and isolating your decision making during an emergency will also help you slow
down any pending decisions. This gives you the time and space necessary to strategically align
your current thoughts and actions with your immediate and long-term goals.
Under stress, people tend to make reactive decisions. Reactive change isn’t necessarily a bad
thing but it is only valuable when you have spent the time, energy, and training to react in a
calculated and programmed way, and in a way that will move you towards the outcome you
want. Sometimes, that means sacrificing short term goals for long term existence.
Trained business operators have a systematic, real time process to formulate the proper response
to an emergency. To the inexperienced business owner or leader, it may not be clear what is
occurring, and it may still look like chaos. But to the trained and experienced eye, all the activity
is executed in a swift, disciplined process.
When you ask someone who is trained and experienced in dealing with crises, they can

Compare that to most business owners, and you’ll see they have never trained or prepared this

find themselves in a crisis, self-inflicted or not, most often they freeze, or flail around while trying
to fight to survive, or make irrational decisions, which usually make things worse. An ill
prepared team is a recipe for disaster, at all times.
Unfortunately, when things are going well, most business leaders place little value on training
and preparing for emergencies. Obviously, it is difficult to prepare for all types of situations, and
sometimes you have to jump into action to solve an immediate problem. Knowing when to stop
taking action and not overreact is a vital skill, but one usually gained only through training and
experience. During times of fear, stress, and anxiety, taking no action or improper actions makes
the situation worse, both in the short and long-term.
The purpose of this book is to help you find the courage to take the correct actions by using my
recommendations, building your confidence in your decision making, and preparing yourself to
make the right decisions at the right time.
I want to help you through times of emergency or crisis by giving you a methodology that will

data, and take actions that will protect your business in the current moment, while preserving
your long-term vision.

It’s important to understand that your future is the total sum of every moment you experience,
even during an emergency. To best understand your future trajectory, you must pay attention to
the conversations you’re having and actions you’re taking today.
Your future results follow your current actions. Your current actions follow your current words.
Your current words follow your current thoughts.
When you encounter resistance in life, it's usually because you are acting on faulty information
from bad advice, misplaced intentions, inexperience, or past experiences that resulted in poor
results. But these thoughts, words used, actions will define you and your legacy. The more clarity
you have about what you want your ultimate legacy to be, the clearer your thinking, words,
actions and results will be. And those results will eventually frame your legacy.
We can understand this through something called the Law of Circulation. It states that all things
in the universe are always flowing in circulation but at an ever-expanding rate. What you give to
one person, you will receive back from a different source. You don’t have to give with the
expectation of receiving something back from the same person, because you know that your
reward will definitely come back to you multiplied from other sources. This works according to
the law of cause and effect, in which the universe always mirrors back to you whatever you do.
In other words, what you proclaim with conviction, put out into the universe, and act on, will
ultimately come back around. The more power you give it, the more significant and profound its
impact will be. When spoken, thoughts produce energy, and actions amplify that energy. The
more power and certainty the energy has today, the more profound its impact will be when it
circulates back around tomorrow.

Exercise: Imagine This
Let’s imagine you are in the telemedicine space. You’ve spent an enormous amount of time and
effort creating a remarkable software product for doctors and patients. But the regulatory issues
around the country have been so restrictive and the rules around medical licensing are so
archaic that you can’t get people to pay attention to your technology.
Nevertheless, you are ready to go to market, but then a crisis hits that immediately creates fear

months. So you shut down operations, shelve your plans, let your team go, and retreat from the
direction you were heading. Fear of failure caused you to preemptively react. Then, the
president of the country comes on the television and says that doctors everywhere need to help
because of an emergency. The only fast and safe solution? Telemedicine.
In an instant, the future of telemedicine has shifted for the better. As it turns out, you didn’t need
to take a long, protracted approach to change all of those rules and regulations, but nonetheless,
you missed the opportunity to dominate the market because you prematurely shut down
operations.

CHAPTER 3

In a crisis, many business owners immediately disconnect from their ideal personal,
professional, and financial goals. A departure from your goals will, in itself, throw you into a
state of emotional emergency.
They start questioning and reassessing them, questioning themselves, wondering if they should
have even established such lofty goals in the first place. Then, they think of all the decisions
they made and start wondering why they didn’t make different decisions that would have
protected them from their current situation.
To understand this in context, we’re going to talk about the past-self, present-self, and future-self.
When you understand these selves, you’ll be better prepared in an emergency to continue
moving forward towards your ideal PPF future picture. But do so, you have to prepare the
foundational elements today. Your future results depend on every granular decision and action
you take, starting now. A pragmatic versus emotional perspective will serve you better in both
the short and long-term. The more control you have over your thinking, the more power you
will have over the decisions and actions you take.
Perspective is the best recipe for great decision making. If all of your thoughts and actions up
until now have been foundational to your overall success, a crisis could be the single largest gift
ever given to you.
Why? Because when things are going well, it's easy to mask problems. it's easy to ignore
the lack of foundational structure. If you have underlying problems and a weak
foundation, a future crisis will likely trigger a big collapse. Or it could become the
biggest opportunity you'll ever see for growth
However, you have to step back, review the decisions you’re past-self made, reaffirm
what your ideal future success looks like, and take the present moment thinking to be
sure you take action consistent with moving towards your future picture. I call this taking action
with intent. It's like throwing a stone in the middle of the water. Eventually the ripple effect will
make it back to shore.

The Blackjack Drill
In this section I’m going to talk about how to quickly determine the course of action you need to
take. This drill is critical to your success. Staying power is everything in business. To understand
why, let’s use blackjack as an analogy to a business in crisis.
If you walk up to a $100 blackjack table with only $1,000, you have to bet at least $100 per
hand. If you sit and lose five hands in a row, you’d be on a negative momentum swing.
Therefore, the probability of you winning over the next few hands would be slim, and if you
kept playing at $100 per hand, you would likely run out of money quickly. But if you get up
from the table and move over to a $25 table, you might have the staying power to rebuild your
chip stack and start over. Then again, if you continue to lose at the $25 table, you can move to a
$10 table, hoping to build a winning streak, and eventually get more money than you came in
with. Once that happens, you can start over back at the $100 table.
The reason I use this analogy is to show that it takes a lot of discipline to get up and move
around. But one thing is certain. Once you are out of chips, you no longer have any chance to
win your money back. Done is done. Finished, over, kaput. In blackjack, just like business, if
you don’t make the right choices and position yourself to fight another day, when it's over, it's
over. Finished.
So, let's walk through the process of how to make the right decisions in real time so that you can
stay in the game and wait for the perfect moment to get back on a winning streak. It's about
staying power at this point; it's not about being all-in or all-out. The focus needs to be on buying
time to pounce at the perfect moment.
Here’s how to ensure you can stay in the game as long as possible:
● Project out your revenue for however long you can: daily, one-week, two-weeks, one
month, two months, and so on. This will vary based on the type of business you have.
● Calculate your monthly fixed expenses and cash burn rate. Identify your largest expenses:
people, rent, travel, product costs, marketing spend, entertainment, third party contracts,
etc.
● Find your breakeven point. This is when revenues and costs intersect, and you stop
making money.
● Calculate how much cash you have in cash reserves and make that your sole focus. Protect
and increase your cash reserves.

This process is known as a “breakeven and cash burn analysis.” The point is to determine how
many expenses you must cut, sales you must create, or what combination of those factors

1. Generate revenue greater than your expenses.
2. Cut your expenses to match your revenues to break even.
3. Generate revenue, cut expenses, and protect your cash reserves, if you
have any.
Doing this with discipline will allow you and your team to know exactly what your staying power

Let's frame this. You're the captain of a ship and have 15 employees, and you just hit a rock.
Your ship is going to sink. You say, “Hey team, we just hit a rock. We’re in rough seas and the
ship is going to sink. That’s the bad news. The good news is that we have a life raft that will
hold 15 people, including me. All of us can get into the life raft."
Unfortunately, more bad news comes. Your team realizes that when you hit the rock, the life
raft took some damage as well. It now has a bunch of holes, and some of your people are going
to have to plug the holes with their fingers and toes, while others are going to have to row as fast
as they can. Finally, some are going to have to bail water furiously.
However, you are the captain and you have been here before. Maybe you’re the only one who
has. Unlike sea captains, business owners can’t go down with the ship, because when the
business owner goes down, everyone loses. If you have 20 people and you only have room for
15, you have to use the forced ranking process we teach you later in this book to determine who
to take with you. Trust me, when your ship is going down, making logical, fact-based decisions
fast and as accurately as possible will be key to your survival.
But now you only have room for fifteen people, and as the water trickles into the boat, you tell
your team they have one of three options. They can plug holes, row faster, or bail more
effectively. At some point, you say, if you are not doing one of those three things, if you are
expending any energy doing anything else like distracting coworkers, talking about almost
dying, or wishing that none of this ever happened, the rest of us are going to throw your ass
overboard.
I hate to say it, but it’s obvious as the water fills you have to bail faster or lighten the load. The
more buoyant this life raft is, the better. Your people are either going to be bailing, plugging, or
rowing. If anyone is not doing one of those three things with every ounce of effort they have,
they're out, so that the ones who are working hard can survive.

transparency, and results need to be at the forefront of all your communications. Those who are
a drag on the system are going to be removed in favor of those who are putting their effort in.
That said, if you throw everybody out of the boat, who are you going to have to plug, bail or

preverbal anchor (friends, family, long-term employees that underperform) is all the inspiration a

complete commitment.” Then you are delivering a message with inspiration: we all have to get

Therefore, for every dollar we go under the breakeven, we're going to reduce everyone's income
to the same percentage of every dollar we go under. For example, if our breakeven is $200k and
we do $150k, that’s $50k or 25 percent less. So, everyone’s salaries get reduced by 25 percent.

During an emergency, you’ll establish a new norm and set the standard for the next chapter by

The Emergency Business Response for marketing and sales teams starts with a startup

CHAPTER 12
Here’s the thing. An emergency is not the time to fight for new customers. It’s the time to
retain the ones you already have. Go to your power base, lean into your contacts. Friends,
family, customers, venders, competitors. It's time to fortify relationships.
At Cardone Ventures, when an emergency strikes, the first thing we do is reach out to our
clients individually, calling each business owner and checking in. We offer support and make
sure any questions or concerns have become our priorities as we fight alongside them to keep
the business solid and thriving.
I often teach that the traditional cycle of marketing is flow, conversion, and retention.
Typically, and chronologically, you can think of flow as attracting awareness via
content and platforms. Conversion is the ability to engage with your brand’s messaging
and close deals with new prospects. Then, finally, retention is the efforts you take to
super serve and over deliver for your current customer base while creating new
opportunities.
When a crisis upends your business, you must immediately resort to a retention-first strategy.
You should focus on the guaranteed, low-hanging fruit (like happy current customers). After
all, you've put a significant amount of effort into building those relationships. You should
start there to protect and fortify the business and tap into those relationships to jumpstart a
new cycle. When the economy and your business are in distress, it's time to super serve that
core base of clients, get referrals from them, and remind them why they trusted you in the
first place.
In fact, you should segment your most high valued, loyal, and profitable customers
and communicate directly with them. Staying alive and fighting for another day may require
street fighting in business terms. Find any way possible to pull your future revenues forward.
Don't immediately start discounting your current products and services. Reach out to
loyal customers and offer packages for prepayment. If you’re going to reward
someone, reward your loyal, deserving customers who are already advocates of your
business. Give referral incentives to them for sending new customers to you. Your best
salesperson is always a delighted loyal customer. Crush those relationships with value, value,
value.
When you’re striving to survive, you need to think of your business objectives a little
differently. You need to generate cash and pull revenues forward by offering purchasing
incentives that drive cash into the business. Remember, cash is always king, but in a crisis or
emergency, cash is King Kong. Your business objective is to stockpile enough cash so that
you have staying power in the new game of staying alive and position yourself for long-term
growth. If you end up not needing the cash, you can always use it to buy a weaker
competitor on the rebound.

especially important. Set your team up for success. The more detail you give them about where
the company needs to be and the more you align their daily roles to fight for sales and cash, the
more likely you are to succeed.
Here is what I do. I take a white board and write down each month’s revenues and break even
points. I call this the waterline. I explain that our revenues need to stay above the waterline, and
if they do, there will be no changes to compensation. This, of course, is if there is enough flow
and conversion (opportunities and sales) in the pipeline.
If not, then I explain the waterfall process of decision making. Let's assume in this example, the
waterline is supported by flow and conversion opportunities. I would tell the team that
once the revenues fall below the waterline number, we will be reducing our compensation
by the same percentage. Therefore, if we fall 10 percent below the waterline, everyone is being
reduced by 10 percent in pay. This will continue until our revenues again reach the waterline.
I then explain to every employee that they are all responsible for creating revenue in
everything we do. In addition, each week everyone needs to introduce revenue ideas, find
cross sell opportunities, and help in any way possible, including making sales calls. There
are no excuses, no complaining, and no exceptions.

Crisis Communication
Perception is reality. That’s not just a catchy saying. It’s filled with truth. What your brand
does during a crisis shapes the consumers’ view of who you are and what you stand for.
Letting your phones and digital and traditional brand channels go silent is a surefire way to
watch your clients and potential clients evaporate into thin air.
That being said, you don’t want to communicate just for communication’s sake.
