1. So obvious. Don’t mail lists you have
not checked or that are not ‘opted-in’
Who you are talking to matters much more than what you say. And because people move
addresses and change jobs, lists decay.
Years ago a retailer I knew checked the names and addresses on a compiled list that was
allegedly bang up to date: 20% were wrong.
This was actually not that bad.
Later, a friend who specializes in telemarketing to businesses checked a selection of lists
and found that in some cases as many as 80% of names and addresses were not accurate.
List decay – like everything else – is accelerated online. People change their e-mail
addresses almost as a matter of course, so you have to be even more careful. Steer clear of lists
that haven’t opted-in to hear from you or your affiliates.
A lady in my office does nothing but check names, titles, addresses and e-mail addresses.
If you sell to businesses that may pay for you.

5. Make people “earn” a premium
Anything you have to work for is worth more than something you get without trying. What
is more, most of us think nothing in life is really free.
So make the customer do something to get the premium. Generally that will improve your
response. One obvious requirement is that the customer has to buy a quantity of the product.
(That was the case with the gardening example given above.)
In one case a single line in 10 point bold type in a full page advertisement for records
offering a gift for speedy orders increased response by 7%.
Some people find phone orders are of better quality than postal ones. It certainly pays to
offer people a premium for ordering on the phone. Not only does it get a speedy response, it
attracts people who are interested right then, but might never act if they don't do so immediately.
And giving a number number people can send an SMS to can increase response by 30%.

6. Try more than one premium
Why settle for one premium? You can try a whole series, where the customer has to fulfil
a requirement for each premium.
For example, you get one for buying, an extra one for buying within 7 days (one of our
clients offers a “free mystery gift”) another for buying two, and yet another for buying a de-luxe
version or signing a direct debit on your bank – and so on.
The reason for doing this is simple. Once prospects decide to buy, the flood gates are
open. There isn’t a better time to sell more to them.
Really.
That’s why online you’ll see immediate
up-sell, during or after the initial sale. A good
example is Amazon. The minute they sell you
something, they try to sell you something else.
And what premium do they offer? Not
just one; a few, starting with free delivery.

7. Segment premia
Always base your propositions on the nature and behaviour of your customer.
Thus, by looking at your database you can often see that a certain percentage has an
average order value up to a certain level, others spend more and so on.
It makes sense to have different premia for people to drive them up the levels step by step
– and reward those who buy most. Having the same premium for everybody is the opposite of
good targeted marketing.

8. Scale down the offer... and the
commitment
Sometimes we assume too easily that we must always improve the offers in a sequence.
E.g. if 20% doesn't work ... then we try 30% and so on. Our thinking is: if the prospect won't
accept this offer, I'll make him a better one, till he gives in.
But often the problem for your buyer is not 'how good is the offer?' It could just as easily
be 'How little must I commit myself?'
One publisher had a sequence of messages which started with the best offer first. The
discount was very big ... but the reader had to commit himself for a long period.
And in fact the copy said something like, “If you’re waiting for a better offer, don’t bother.
This is the best deal you’ll ever get.”
Later, 'worse' offers were made. The discount was less, but so was the period of
commitment. So the reader felt easier in his mind. It’s all about getting your prospect to act. If
they are not comfortable spending £300, make sure you have a £100 alternative waiting in the
wings.
You see this online all the time: a cheap entry level, all the way to the super deluxe gold
plated version (more on this later). Fence sitters can happily buy the entry level product (and
later be sold something more expensive) other prospects can happily spend as much they want.
This also works with subscription sites. When you go to the cancellation page they often
offer a substantial discount if you stay rather than end your subscription.

10.Try a de-luxe version
Perhaps the easiest way to increase your margin is to introduce a deluxe or improved
version of the product with a proportionately higher margin for profit.
Almost invariably some percentage of your customers – probably 20-30% – would prefer
the deluxe model, and will pay disproportionately extra for it.
This principle can be applied further when you introduce three levels of option – common
in the insurance business. You can give all sorts of titles – bronze, silver and gold, etc. – but the
principle usually works.

13. Encourage complaints and make more
money
There's an old salesman's saying: 'The sale only starts when the customer says no'.
The psychology is that if somebody cares enough to say no, then they're interested.
Indeed, research has repeatedly shown that a customer who has complained and been satisfied is
more loyal than average.
So don't ignore complaints. Make it easier for people if you can. Nearly all major
manufacturers now have free help lines on their packs.
Your telephone people should be trained not merely to satisfy the complainant, but to sell
alternatives.
I discovered how effective this could be 30 years ago. A client sent money back to people
who complained, plus a long letter offering a much better (and more expensive) product.
So many bought at what was actually a much higher margin, that the final profit was
greater than it would have been if the complaining customers had kept the original product.
The Jay Norris Corporation, a major marketer in the U.S. for many years, used to send
back letters when apologising for delays in delivery offering, on the reverse, a choice of other
products to buy whilst waiting for the original. It worked.

18. Play with size
You can miniaturise your letter or your envelope. Or use tiny type.
The effect is greater if you play upon the shrinking of the message in the copy – e.g. 'Just a
small reminder ...'
The reverse may work. A big envelope was used very effectively a while ago to introduce
a large TV screen.
However, do not assume putting your message in a larger envelope will increase attention
value or results. In most tests I have conducted, a larger envelope has not increased response –
indeed often the reverse. And, of course, it is more expensive.
One company has also conducted tests using that old standby, a big number in the
headline larger than the rest of the type. It didn't make the art director very happy – but it
worked.
Big typewriter face (like Courier) used at the beginning of a letter can grab attention.
An insurance company noticed that the larger the application form in ads and mailings,
the higher the response. The same proved true of the comparison table which shows what you
pay and what you get.
Giving more space and details on how to fill in your order or application form should
usually increase response.
Sometimes the sheer size of a piece of paper will help. Merchandise companies often find
that when the broadsheet unfolds to be very large, the response goes up.

29.Learn what makes people reply

31. Remember the golden rules of
testing
The late Richard V. Benson said there are only two rules in direct marketing.
Rule 1: Test everything. Rule 2: Refer to rule 1.
This is true. But saying “we must test” is one thing. There are other things to remember
which I cover in my book Commonsense Direct and Digital Marketing.
First, there are two kinds of test: the kind where you test one approach against another;
and the kind where you test on small numbers before you invest in large numbers.
Both are vitally important. Both are grossly neglected – especially the second. People are
constantly banging things out in huge numbers without pre-testing.
They do this, especially online, because it’s cheap.
What is expensive (and ignored) is what it costs in long term damage.
A failed message is not just bad because you don’t make money.
It is bad because a) you wasted an opportunity, and wasted money in doing so, and b) you
made your prospects just that little (maybe a lot) less likely to reply next time.
When you are testing one approach against another, there are two different ways to go.
If you vary one element in your message, you know precisely how much difference it
made. If you change more than one, you don’t; so might as well change everything.
So, test one, or test all.
Lastly, make sure your tests are statistically viable. Unless the numbers are right, the
results will be wrong.
